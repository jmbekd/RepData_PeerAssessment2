Human Health and Economic Impacts of Various Weather Events Across the United States: 1996 to 2011

Synopsis

In this analysis, the United States (“U.S.”) National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration’s (“NOAA”) storm database was used to assess the human health and economic impacts of weather events across the U.S. After downloading the data, the data were loaded into R 3.1.1 and the RStudio 0.98.953 integrated development environment (“IDE”). After conducting some exploratory data analysis and additional online research, the dataset was subset to only include data collected between 1996 and 2011. Data munging was performed to standardize the listed weather event types in the database with the list of Permitted Storm Data Events as outlined in Table 1 of the Storm Data Preparation publication. Human health impacts were assessed by adding the number of injuries and fatalities for each weather event type and economic impacts were assessed by adding the property damage costs and crop damage costs for each weather event type. Based on this analysis, the top 5 weather events (listed in decreasing order) that caused the greatest number of injuries and fatalities are tornadoes, excessive heat, floods, thunderstorm winds, and lightning. Based on this analysis, the top 5 weather events (listed in decreasing order) in terms of economic impacts are floods, hurricanes (typhoons), storm surge/tidal events, tornadoes, and hail. Due to budget and time constraints, additional analysis of this dataset was not performed; should additional resources be procured for this project, subsequent analyses should attempt to identify outliers that may have skewed this analysis and ascertain whether there is are any temporal trends in the number of reported events and/or the types of reported events.

According to the Storm Data Preparation publication, the damage estimates (“PROPDMG” and “CROPDMG”) should be rounded to three significant digits and followed by an alphabetical character (“PROPDMGEXP” and “CROPDMGEXP”) signifying the magnitude of the number. In exploring the data, it was noted that in addition to “h”, “k”, “m”, and “b” (signifying hundreds, thousands, millions, and billions, respectively), there were also numeric values between 0 and 9, empty strings (i.e., “”), and other characters such as “+”, “-”, “?” within the “PROPDMGEXP” and “CROPDMGEXP” columns. An online publication appears to indicate that the numeric values between 0 and 9 may have been damage intervals. The maximum values of each of the damage intervals are:

Damage Interval Maximum Damage
0 NA
1 $50
2 $500
3 $5,000
4 $50,000
5 $500,000
6 $5,000,000
7 $50,000,000

Using the information above, the property and crop damage estimates were calculated using the code below. In the event that a numeric value was included in the “PROPDMG” or “CROPDMG” columns and a numeric value was also included in the “PROPDMGEXP” and “CROPDMGEXP” columns, the value in the “PROPDMG” or “CROPDMG” column was ignored and the damage interval value was used as the damage estimate. After calculating the individual property and crop damage estimates, they were summed together (removing NAs prior to summing) to obtain the total cost (i.e., economic impact) of each individual weather event.

## Convert estimated property and crop damage columns by converting the letter factors in the PROPDMGEXP and CROPDMGEXP 
## columns (i.e., h, k, m, and b) to their respective numeric values (using a damage conversion vector) and 
## multiplying by the numeric values in the PROPDMG and CROPDMG columns, respectively. This block of code also converts 
## the numeric damage intervals in the PROPDMGEXP and CROPDMGEXP columns (i.e., 1 through 9) to their respective maximum
## values as laid out in the publication at: http://sciencepolicy.colorado.edu/admin/publication_files/2012.31.pdf
## If a numeric value is present in both the XXXXDMG and XXXXDMGEXP columns, it was assumed that the numeric damage 
## interval in the XXXXDMGEXP column is correct and the value in the XXXXDMG column is ignored.
## Values of "", "+", "-", "?", and "0" were all coded as NAs. After converting/calculating the property and crop damage,  
## the total cost of each event is calculated by adding the property damages and the crop damages together.
dmg_mult <- c("h" = 100, "k" = 1000, "m" = 1000000, "b" = 1000000000) # damage conversion vector

setkey(dmg, PROPDMGEXP) 
dmg[J(c("h", "k", "m", "b")), prop_cost := PROPDMG * dmg_mult[names(dmg_mult) == PROPDMGEXP]]

As can be seen, the top 5 weather events (listed in decreasing order) in terms of economic impacts are floods, hurricanes (typhoons), storm surge/tidal events, tornadoes, and hail. The observation that floods have resulted in greater property and crop damages than hurricanes is somewhat surprising and in conflict with NOAA’s peer-refereed 2013 publication entitled U.S. Billion-Dollar Weather and Climate Disasters: Data Sources, Trends, Accuracy, and Biases and may indicate that:

  • some incorrect assumptions and/or calculations may have been made in this analysis; or,
  • there may be outliers present in the dataset.

Due to budget and time constraints, additional investigation of this dataset was not performed.
